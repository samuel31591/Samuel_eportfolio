Information Risk Management

This module discusses the concepts and principles of information risk management (IRM). An examination of both traditional and modern software development life cycle (SDLC) models is provided, focusing on the areas most impacted by risk management concerns. The course will give an understanding of IRM principles and a review of assessment and mitigation techniques. 

  • Knowledge of the fundamental principles of information risk management.

  • A knowledge of the relationship between IRM and SDLC.

  • An understanding of the SDLC's role in risk management and residual risk.

  • An knowledge of current and future challenges, constraints, and opportunities.

    • Knowledgeable in identifying and critically analysing IT system risks and problems, as well as determining effective processes, tools, and techniques for resolving or mitigating them.

    • Ability to assess and develop programmes and systems in order to provide a solution that meets the design specification.

    • Capability to critically analyse and evaluate produced solutions.

    • Ability to develop and implement skills required to be an effective member of a development team in a virtual professional environment, while adopting real-life perspectives on roles and team organisation.

    Security is a crucial part of any application or environment (Bullock and Benford, 1999) and granting access to these secured applications and the environment needs to be controlled; who gets access to what? What are the rules? How is it tracked? These are managed through an access control system providing access to restricted areas of the business (SailPoint, 2021).” Access control is governed according to the space within which subjects and objects reside, and the ability to traverse space to get close to an object” by Bullock and Benford, 1999. Who(subject) gets access to resources(object) is validated through authentication and authorisation processes.

    Authentication, verifying who someone is, has long passed username and passwords to more advanced Artificial intelligence and deep learning technologies. The application of multi-factor authentication verifying an individual identity based on two to three different parameters, something you (have, know and are) works based on artificial intelligence and deep learning, where data, logs, transactions, and real-time communications are analysed to detect an intruder (Okta, 2018).

    Hardware embedded chips are evolving technologies that authenticate users using multiple levels and methods. An example is the sixth generation vPro Chips by intel (IFF Lab, 2019).

    Authorisation is to which asset one has permission to access through settings supported by security teams (Okta, 2018). The more often used to perform these authorisation processes include Role-Based Access Control, Discretionary based access control, Attribute-based access control, Rule-Based Access Control. These strategies help application developers control the access to the resources depending on the applied method and to what level the user is permitted.

    Authentication and authorisation are vital in access control management, verifying a user’s identity effectively, thus allowing authenticated users to access protected resources depending on the given authority level; these ensure that security is maintained both in the network and the software to be accessed (Wang, Bo and Zhang, 2013).

    Hi, I agree with your post. Firstly, the importance of authentication and authorisation is well documented. In 2020, the most common password was still 123456 (Fruhlinger & Martin, 2021; Whitney, 2020) which shows that the message still is not getting through. Users are not protecting themselves adequately, and systems do not provide sufficient rules for creating secure passwords. But is the onus on the system owners or the users themselves to ensure appropriate standards when authenticating? In many cases, the organisation will be required to put specific standards in place, however I believe that education for users, or at least uptake, is still also lacking and therefore more still needs to be done. As you have alluded, a single mode of authentication may not be enough, and multifactor authentication (using various types) in particular is a very good method of protecting user identity. In a digital world where almost anything can be spoofed, having multiple forms of authentication makes it exponentially more difficult to fake an identity, both when carrying out transactions and authenticating to systems. Artificial intelligence may well be the way forward, together with attribute-based authentication which takes many factors into account during the authentication process. In terms of authorisation, following the principle of least privilege, whereby a subject is only given the minimum permissions or privileges to do any specific task, can protect against malicious or accidental misuse, and help to limit the effects of advanced persistent threats (APT). OWASP provides authentication and authorisation cheat sheets (OWASP, N.D.a; OWASP, N.D.b), which provides best practice ideas for system designers

    Post by Nitin Subramaniam;Peer response

    I agree with your post, Samuel. As Richard pointed out, the most common password is still 123456. It is clear that passwords create risk and in order for enterprises to truly protect their systems, apps, and data, all requests for access to corporate resources must include more than just a user name and password. A key tenet of the Zero Trust model is strong access management. Businesses may increase security and improve the user experience by switching to a modern Identity and Access Management solution that includes capabilities like passwordless authentication and adaptive MFA. Secure access in Zero Trust Model would look like this: Do I know the user? Have I seen this device before? Does the device meet our standards? What is our risk tolerance? Should we grant or deny access? Adaptive MFA provides contextual information and can determine which authentication factors apply to a particular situation. Users may be required to only accept a push notification if the situation is deemed less risky, or maybe enter an SMS code also if the situation is deemed medium risky and altogether denied if the situation is deemed high risk.

    Hi Samuel, this is an important security feature. Authentication and Authorization require Identification features. Passwords are a means of Identification provided mostly at the application layer. Initially, password Identification would require a 4-digit numeric number but nowadays, these are considered weak. Applications like the Google password Manager can generate strong passwords with a combination of numeric, alphanumeric and special characters. There are also advance identification methods like biometric identification which employs user's physical attributes like fingerprint, iris, retina, faces and voice. Advancements now incorporate multibiometric schemes for user authentication (Luis-Garcia et al., 2003). Authentication and Authorization will keep advancing depending on how the identification features evolve. As you mentioned, identifying who someone is, has long passed username and passwords to more advanced Artificial intelligence and deep learning technologies. There is also the multi-factor authentication, that is, verifying an individual identity based on two to three different parameters, something you have, know and are. This can therefore be inferred that the potential in this security feature, will not be exhausted anytime soon as long as there are unique and differing attributes in life. In my opinion, the challenge may only lie on the ability to integrate new identification features into Authorization and Authentication.
