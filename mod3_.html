Information Risk Management

This module discusses the concepts and principles of information risk management (IRM). An examination of both traditional and modern software development life cycle (SDLC) models is provided, focusing on the areas most impacted by risk management concerns. The course will give an understanding of IRM principles and a review of assessment and mitigation techniques. 

  • Knowledge of the fundamental principles of information risk management.

  • A knowledge of the relationship between IRM and SDLC.

  • An understanding of the SDLC's role in risk management and residual risk.

  • An knowledge of current and future challenges, constraints, and opportunities.

    • Knowledgeable in identifying and critically analysing IT system risks and problems, as well as determining effective processes, tools, and techniques for resolving or mitigating them.

    • Ability to assess and develop programmes and systems in order to provide a solution that meets the design specification.

    • Capability to critically analyse and evaluate produced solutions.

    • Ability to develop and implement skills required to be an effective member of a development team in a virtual professional environment, while adopting real-life perspectives on roles and team organisation.

    The article's authors discussed whether user participation is necessary for security risk management (SRM) using a qualitative and quantitative approach. The qualitative approach gathered information on the activities, behaviours, and assignments defining user participation. They then used quantitative methods to assess the model.

    User participation in SRM was found to raise organisational consciousness of security risks and controls within targeted business processes and eased the greater alignment of SRM with business objectives, values, and needs. User participation creates awareness of security risks and controls within targeted business processes. As a result, the development and performance of security controls will improve (Spears and Barki, 2010).

    IS security literature has often cited users as the weak link in IS security due to user errors and negligence. The present study provides empirical evidence that suggests otherwise. User participation raises organisational awareness of security risks and controls within business processes. The need for regulatory compliance may encourage user participation in SRM. Security managers can use regulatory compliance to engage users, raise organisational awareness of security, and better align security measures with business objectives.

    Post by Isioma Vina Okafor-Tolofari

    Regarding the two methods complementing each other, In quantitative analysis, risks are sorted by their financial impact, assets by their financial value, and the result is expressed in a specific management terminology. The evaluation and results are based on objective methods. In contrast, the qualitative approach makes it easier to understand and observe the level of risk, with the methods of calculation being simple and easy to implement. The frequency of occurrence of threats does not need to be quantified as the monetary value of information is not determined, which makes the analysis easier. Therefore quantitative calculations of frequency and impact are not necessary.

    They both have their drawbacks. In the quantitative approach, the value of the risk impact is based on the subjective opinion of the people involved. The process takes a long time, and the results are presented only in monetary value and are hard to understand by people without experience. The process is complex. Unlike the qualitative approach, a cost-benefit analysis is not implemented, only a subjective approach of the author, which makes it challenging to implement controls. There is insufficient differentiation between major risks, and the result depends on the quality of the management team.

    INTRODUCTION

    This report will examine three enterprise resource planning (ERP) solutions that could potentially satisfy Acme’s Manufacturing needs; a commercial-off-the-shelf (COTS), an open-source, and an in-house created solution.

    ASSUMPTIONS

    Financial Status and Stability

    Acme’s net profit is £25 million and has an annual turnover of £15 million (Table 1.1 & Appendix A). The company has financial stability ensured due to building and maintaining customer relationships, managing cash flow, bills, and debts (Mazars, 2021).

    RISK APPETITE

    Acme maintains strict controls to protect its technology infrastructure from external threats and has zero tolerance for unauthorised access to systems and confidential data that could potentially lead to business continuity termination (Reciprocity, 2022).

    IT CAPABILITIES

    Acme can identify IT needs, improve business processes; aiming to do so cost-effectively, and supply constant maintenance and support for IT-based systems (Karimi et. al., 2007). In addition, CompTIA (2019) also published a report identifying the latest SME trends in technology that highlight SMEs' decisions and perceptions of technology relevant to business objectives (See Infographic 1).

    STATUS

    The company follows a functional departmentalisation with five departments: retail operations, manufacturing and sourcing, logistics, operations and product design (Weedmark, 2019). SMEs often use this type of company structure (Kenton, 2021a). Acme being a SME, uses Excel Spreadsheets for accounting needs, resource planning and management (Financial Post, 2021).

    BUSINESS RISKS

    Some of the key business risks that Acme might have to overcome are strategic, compliance/regulatory, financial, operational, and reputational risks (NI Business Info, N.D.a; Kenton, 2022).

    STRATEGIC RISK

    Introduced due to directors’ fundamental decisions on the business’s objectives (ACCA, 2022).

    COMPLIANCE RISK

    Appears when businesses violate established laws and/or regulations (Hayes, 2022).

    Regulatory Risk

    Arises when laws and regulations change, leading to security, business, sector, or market being impacted by these changes (Hayes, 2022).

    Financial Risk

    Instabilities and losses in the market, movements in stock prices, currencies, etcetera can be reasons financial risk arises (NI Business Info, N.D.b).

    Operational Risk

    Arises from business’s uncertainties and hazards when attempting to complete daily activities within a given field or industry (Segal, 2020).

    Reputational Risk

    Considered a threat or danger to a business’s good name or standing (Kenton, 2021b).

    Security Risk

    "arise from the loss of confidentiality, integrity, or availability of information or information systems” (NIST, 2021).

    Conclusion

    This report evaluates the three ERP solutions; commercial-off-the-shelf (COTS), open-source, and in-house, based on key elements in the framework, including the involvement of organisational levels, critical success factors, implementation methodology and process, and relevant risks. COTS solutions are considered to be more suitable for SMEs and easier to manage, as well as projects to replace the company's deprecated systems, making them more efficient, and keeping customers satisfied (Bank for Canadian Entrepreneurs, N.D.; Glenn & Shyam, 2004).

    As Acme Manufacturing is a SME and based on this report, COTS solution seems to satisfy the company’s requirements and needs (see Appendix A). Although each solution is followed by risks, COTS offers adaptability, flexibility, potential technical upgradeability, and integration of the latest technologies from the proposed framework (Anderson, 2021).

    From the journal, the five overall causes of risk, as highlighted by the article by Roy et al. (2015) and Hijazi et al. (2014) that I can deduce are;

    1.Technological - These are the project’s technical implementation risks. Examples of this risk are risk introduced by the code, old legacy code, technological debt, or selecting the wrong technology.

    2.Operating - These are organisational risks, like downtime caused by unreasonable searches for specific specialists for project creation and poor project management at the various phases of SDLC (Khdour et al., 2014). The risk here can be classified as operational.

    3.Business - The risks faced by businesses are a combination of positive and negative events that directly affect business growth and solvency. A company going bankrupt due to debt is an example.

    4.External - External risks can affect the business and, in turn, the project it is working on, positively or negatively. An example of such an external risk is the recent pandemic, which is unprecedented and unpredictable.

    5.Improper management of changes - risks arising from relatively long stages from one state of development to the other makes it difficult to estimate the time cost and other resources needed to accomplish each stage efficiently.

    Security is a crucial part of any application or environment (Bullock and Benford, 1999) and granting access to these secured applications and the environment needs to be controlled; who gets access to what? What are the rules? How is it tracked? These are managed through an access control system providing access to restricted areas of the business (SailPoint, 2021).” Access control is governed according to the space within which subjects and objects reside, and the ability to traverse space to get close to an object” by Bullock and Benford, 1999. Who(subject) gets access to resources(object) is validated through authentication and authorisation processes.

    Authentication, verifying who someone is, has long passed username and passwords to more advanced Artificial intelligence and deep learning technologies. The application of multi-factor authentication verifying an individual identity based on two to three different parameters, something you (have, know and are) works based on artificial intelligence and deep learning, where data, logs, transactions, and real-time communications are analysed to detect an intruder (Okta, 2018).

    Hardware embedded chips are evolving technologies that authenticate users using multiple levels and methods. An example is the sixth generation vPro Chips by intel (IFF Lab, 2019).

    Authorisation is to which asset one has permission to access through settings supported by security teams (Okta, 2018). The more often used to perform these authorisation processes include Role-Based Access Control, Discretionary based access control, Attribute-based access control, Rule-Based Access Control. These strategies help application developers control the access to the resources depending on the applied method and to what level the user is permitted.

    Authentication and authorisation are vital in access control management, verifying a user’s identity effectively, thus allowing authenticated users to access protected resources depending on the given authority level; these ensure that security is maintained both in the network and the software to be accessed (Wang, Bo and Zhang, 2013).

In a digital world where almost anything can be spoofed, having multiple forms of authentication makes it exponentially more difficult to fake an identity, both when carrying out transactions and authenticating to systems. Artificial intelligence may well be the way forward, together with attribute-based authentication which takes many factors into account during the authentication process. In terms of authorisation, following the principle of least privilege, whereby a subject is only given the minimum permissions or privileges to do any specific task, can protect against malicious or accidental misuse, and help to limit the effects of advanced persistent threats (APT).
