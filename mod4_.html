Secure Software Development

In this module the concept of a secure software development lifecycle will be introduced as well as the security risks associated with programming languages from the point of view of the design approach and architecture, programming paradigms, testing, impacts and implications of the operating system. practices and libraries for software development, security risks associated with distributed systems and APIs, and future trends in secure software and system design.

 

The main focus of the module are

  • understanding of the concept of abstraction in programming.

  • understanding of the basic principles of secure development methodologies.

  • Understanding of analysis, program design, software construction and testing required for software development.

  • understanding of the basic principles of architecture, as well as traditional and contemporary Software Development Life Cycle (SDLC) models, such as TOGAF and Agile.

    Regarding the two methods complementing each other, In quantitative analysis, risks are sorted by their financial impact, assets by their financial value, and the result is expressed in a specific management terminology. The evaluation and results are based on objective methods. In contrast, the qualitative approach makes it easier to understand and observe the level of risk, with the methods of calculation being simple and easy to implement. The frequency of occurrence of threats does not need to be quantified as the monetary value of information is not determined, which makes the analysis easier. Therefore quantitative calculations of frequency and impact are not necessary.

    They both have their drawbacks. In the quantitative approach, the value of the risk impact is based on the subjective opinion of the people involved. The process takes a long time, and the results are presented only in monetary value and are hard to understand by people without experience. The process is complex. Unlike the qualitative approach, a cost-benefit analysis is not implemented, only a subjective approach of the author, which makes it challenging to implement controls. There is insufficient differentiation between major risks, and the result depends on the quality of the management team.

    INTRODUCTION

    This report will examine three enterprise resource planning (ERP) solutions that could potentially satisfy Acme’s Manufacturing needs; a commercial-off-the-shelf (COTS), an open-source, and an in-house created solution.

    ASSUMPTIONS

    Financial Status and Stability

    Acme’s net profit is £25 million and has an annual turnover of £15 million (Table 1.1 & Appendix A). The company has financial stability ensured due to building and maintaining customer relationships, managing cash flow, bills, and debts (Mazars, 2021).

    RISK APPETITE

    Acme maintains strict controls to protect its technology infrastructure from external threats and has zero tolerance for unauthorised access to systems and confidential data that could potentially lead to business continuity termination (Reciprocity, 2022).

    IT CAPABILITIES

    Acme can identify IT needs, improve business processes; aiming to do so cost-effectively, and supply constant maintenance and support for IT-based systems (Karimi et. al., 2007). In addition, CompTIA (2019) also published a report identifying the latest SME trends in technology that highlight SMEs' decisions and perceptions of technology relevant to business objectives (See Infographic 1).

    STATUS

    The company follows a functional departmentalisation with five departments: retail operations, manufacturing and sourcing, logistics, operations and product design (Weedmark, 2019). SMEs often use this type of company structure (Kenton, 2021a). Acme being a SME, uses Excel Spreadsheets for accounting needs, resource planning and management (Financial Post, 2021).

    BUSINESS RISKS

    Some of the key business risks that Acme might have to overcome are strategic, compliance/regulatory, financial, operational, and reputational risks (NI Business Info, N.D.a; Kenton, 2022).

    STRATEGIC RISK

    Introduced due to directors’ fundamental decisions on the business’s objectives (ACCA, 2022).

    COMPLIANCE RISK

    Appears when businesses violate established laws and/or regulations (Hayes, 2022).

    Regulatory Risk

    Arises when laws and regulations change, leading to security, business, sector, or market being impacted by these changes (Hayes, 2022).

    Financial Risk

    Instabilities and losses in the market, movements in stock prices, currencies, etcetera can be reasons financial risk arises (NI Business Info, N.D.b).

    Operational Risk

    Arises from business’s uncertainties and hazards when attempting to complete daily activities within a given field or industry (Segal, 2020).

    Reputational Risk

    Considered a threat or danger to a business’s good name or standing (Kenton, 2021b).

    Security Risk

    "arise from the loss of confidentiality, integrity, or availability of information or information systems” (NIST, 2021).

    Conclusion

    This report evaluates the three ERP solutions; commercial-off-the-shelf (COTS), open-source, and in-house, based on key elements in the framework, including the involvement of organisational levels, critical success factors, implementation methodology and process, and relevant risks. COTS solutions are considered to be more suitable for SMEs and easier to manage, as well as projects to replace the company's deprecated systems, making them more efficient, and keeping customers satisfied (Bank for Canadian Entrepreneurs, N.D.; Glenn & Shyam, 2004).

    As Acme Manufacturing is a SME and based on this report, COTS solution seems to satisfy the company’s requirements and needs (see Appendix A). Although each solution is followed by risks, COTS offers adaptability, flexibility, potential technical upgradeability, and integration of the latest technologies from the proposed framework (Anderson, 2021).

    From the journal, the five overall causes of risk, as highlighted by the article by Roy et al. (2015) and Hijazi et al. (2014) that I can deduce are;

    1.Technological - These are the project’s technical implementation risks. Examples of this risk are risk introduced by the code, old legacy code, technological debt, or selecting the wrong technology.

    2.Operating - These are organisational risks, like downtime caused by unreasonable searches for specific specialists for project creation and poor project management at the various phases of SDLC (Khdour et al., 2014). The risk here can be classified as operational.

    3.Business - The risks faced by businesses are a combination of positive and negative events that directly affect business growth and solvency. A company going bankrupt due to debt is an example.

    4.External - External risks can affect the business and, in turn, the project it is working on, positively or negatively. An example of such an external risk is the recent pandemic, which is unprecedented and unpredictable.

    5.Improper management of changes - risks arising from relatively long stages from one state of development to the other makes it difficult to estimate the time cost and other resources needed to accomplish each stage efficiently.

    How can we accurately represent and account for uncertainties in a way that properly justifies confidence in the risk results?

    MY ANSWER

    Uncertainty is a key concept in risk conceptualisation and risk assessments. Probabilistic analysis is the predominant method used to handle the uncertainties involved in risk analysis, both aleatory (representing variation) and epistemic (due to lack of knowledge). Flage et al. (2014) examines the problem and identify foundational issues for its treatment. If subjective probabilities are used to express the uncertainties, we also need to reflect on the knowledge that supports the probabilities. The NUSAP system (NUSAP: Numeral, Unit, Spread, Assessment, and Pedigree) was initially designed for the analysis and diagnosis of uncertainty in science for policy by performing a critical appraisal of the knowledge base behind the relevant scientific information.

    For example, Funtowicz and Ravetz, 1990, Kloprogge, 2005, Laes and Laes, 2011, van der Sluijs, and Spiegelhalter and Riesch provide forms of expression of uncertainty within five levels: event, parameter and model uncertainty –. For interval probabilities, it is important to consider the background knowledge and strength of this knowledge. For situations characterised by large and "deep" uncertainties, there seems to be broad acceptance of the need to see beyond probability. The combination of probability and qualitative approaches represents an interesting alternative research direction. A central area of uncertainty in risk assessment is uncertainty importance analysis (Aven, 2016)

    Models play an important role in risk assessments, and considerable attention has been devoted to the issue of model uncertainty. The challenge is to identify what are the most critical and essential contributors to output uncertainties and risk. According to Aven and Zio (2013), model uncertainty is interpreted as uncertainty about the model error, defined by g(x) – y, where y is the quantity, we would like to assess and g(x) is a model of y having some parameters x. Nevertheless, there has been some lack of clarity in the risk field regarding what this concept means.

    Question 1

      The NSA list a series of ‘hard problems’ that “require (security) science as a community to focus (on) and measure their progress” (NSA, 2020). These problems concern:

    • Scalability and composability

    • Policy governed secure collaboration

    • Security metrics driven SDLC

    • Resilient architectures

    • Understanding and accounting for human behaviour (NSA, 2020).

      • Which of the above problems involve the use of information risk management? Justify your answer with appropriate references.

      The 5 Hard Problems were developed by the Principal Investigators (PIs) of the Science of Security Lablets in collaboration with NSA Research to establish the beginnings of a common language and gauge progress. They were chosen for their technical difficulty, potential operational significance, and the likelihood of benefiting from a focus on scientific research methods and improved measurement capabilities. The five are not intended to be all-inclusive of everything that needs to be done in cybersecurity but rather five specific areas that need scientific progress (AdamT, 2015).

      Scalability and Composability: create methods for building secure systems with known security properties from components with known security properties without having to re-analyze the constituent elements completely.

      Policy-Governed Secure Collaboration: Create methods for expressing and enforcing normative requirements and policies for handling data with varying usage needs and among users with different levels of authority.

      Security Metrics Driven Evaluation, Design, Development, and Deployment: Create security metrics and models capable of predicting or confirming whether a given cyber system retains a given set of security properties (deterministically or probabilistically) in a given context.

      Resilient Architectures: Create methods for designing and analyzing system architectures that provide required service in the face of compromised components. Understanding.

      Accounting for Human Behavior: Create models of human behaviour (both users and adversaries) that allow for the design, modeling, and analysis of systems with specific security properties (AdamT, 2015).

      Information Risk management is concerned with the identification, evaluation, and prioritisation of risks (defined in ISO 31000 as the effect of uncertainty on objectives) followed by coordinated and economical application of resources to minimise, monitor, and control the probability or impact of unfortunate events or to maximise the realisation of opportunities (Blakley, McDermott and Geer, 2001).

      The five challenging problems collaboratively were chosen for their technical difficulty, potential operational significance, and the likelihood of benefiting from a focus on scientific research methods and improved measurement capabilities in responding to this information risk management and as individually they are not all-inclusive. Hence, every one of them involves the use of information risk management.
